AI Generalisation Gap in Comorbid Sleep Disorder Staging

The finding

A model that reads healthy sleep well fails on the patients who need it most.

55%
Accuracy of an SHHS-trained model when deployed on ischemic-stroke sleep — down from 88% on healthy sleepers. A drop of ≈30 points, exactly where clinical reliability matters.

Automated sleep staging is trained almost entirely on healthy EEG. But the patients who most need overnight monitoring — stroke survivors with comorbid apnea — have profoundly disrupted sleep architecture.

We show this domain gap is real, large, and diagnosable: statistical tests separate the two populations perfectly, and Grad-CAM reveals the healthy-trained model attends to physiologically uninformative EEG regions on patient data — the right answer for the wrong reasons, and often the wrong answer entirely.

Abstract

Accurate sleep staging is essential for diagnosing obstructive sleep apnea and hypopnea in stroke patients. Although polysomnography is reliable, it is costly, labor-intensive, and manually scored. While deep learning enables automated EEG-based sleep staging in healthy subjects, our analysis shows poor generalisation to clinical populations with disrupted sleep. Using Grad-CAM interpretations, we systematically demonstrate this limitation.

We introduce iSLEEPS, a newly clinically annotated ischemic-stroke dataset, and evaluate an SE-ResNet plus bidirectional LSTM model for single-channel EEG sleep staging. As expected, cross-domain performance between healthy and diseased subjects is poor. Attention visualisations, supported by clinical expert feedback, show the model focuses on physiologically uninformative EEG regions in patient data. Statistical and computational analyses further confirm significant sleep-architecture differences between healthy and ischemic-stroke cohorts — highlighting the need for subject-aware or disease-specific models with clinical validation before deployment.

Explainability

Right score, wrong reasons.

On the 100 iSLEEPS patients, the SHHS-trained model scored just 55.1% accuracy (51.9% MF1). To understand why, clinicians reviewed Grad-CAM heatmaps for every epoch of four randomly selected patients.

Despite training on healthy data, the model repeatedly attended to physiologically irrelevant regions — diffuse slowing, intermittent delta bursts, and movement or electrode artifacts — rather than the markers that define sleep stages: spindles, K-complexes, and alpha attenuation. The mismatch was sharpest during N1–N2 transitions and REM, where attention drifted to ischemia-related slowing and hemispheric asymmetry over lesioned areas. A model trained on iSLEEPS, by contrast, locked onto clinically meaningful features.

The statistical lens

Unstable, fragmented sleep

Stroke patients are far more likely to wake from light non-REM sleep than to descend into deep restorative sleep — a signature of frequent arousal and reduced sleep efficiency, consistent with post-stroke disruption of thalamocortical and brainstem arousal systems.

The computational lens

Perfectly separable by structure alone

Given the divergence, we trained binary classifiers — Random Forest, Logistic Regression, Decision Tree, SVM — to tell whether an unepoched recording came from a patient or a healthy subject, using 27 engineered structural and sequential features.

Average Run Length — the mean length of an uninterrupted stretch in one stage — separates the cohorts perfectly. Combinations of stage bigrams, the Shannon entropy of the stage sequence, and maximum run length also hit 100% accuracy with zero AUC-ROC variance across five folds.

Patient nights sprawl across a wide range of run lengths; healthy nights stay tightly constrained (Fig. 5).
